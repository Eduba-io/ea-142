How we would think about that app.

Most of it is not AI. The session-data pipeline from the device to the cloud is traditional code. The scheduling, the insurer reporting, the family notifications: rule-based logic. The part that is genuinely an AI problem is narrow, and it is the physician report. Taking structured session data from a pediatric rehab session and generating a clinically useful, clinician-readable summary that a physical medicine and rehab specialist actually signs off on is the real language-model problem. Everything upstream of that summary is a data and workflow problem that a small team can solve with structure rather than with more engineers.

60%

Ordinary code

Device-to-cloud session pipeline, account and scheduling, insurer exports. Work your team already knows how to build.

30%

Rule-based logic

Family notifications, prescription intake, reimbursement triggers, fit-and-size validation. Postgres and clear rules outperform a model here.

10%

Genuine AI

The physician report. Clinician-readable session summaries a PM&R specialist will sign off on. Narrow scope, real value.

A shape we have seen before.

We helped Feeld's CTO work through a version of this shape in a scoped sprint. Workshop, advisory calls, Organizational Context Architecture, a Strategic Operations Framework his team could keep running after we left. The frame we used, Interpretable Context Methodology, was published in ACM TiiS. It lays out how to organize an agentic system so that clinician-facing language, device telemetry, session protocols, and working artifacts stay separate and legible. The repo is public and MIT-licensed.
